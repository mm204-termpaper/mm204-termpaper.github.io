The function of Healthy Kidneys

The urinary system consists of two kidneys, which filter the blood and deliver urine produced to the two ureters. From the ureters, urine is transferred to the urinary bladder, which is excreted, through the urethra during urination. The kidneys are bean-shaped organs about 11 cm long, 4 to 5 cm wide and 2 to 3 cm wide and lie dormant within the retro peritoneum in the abdomen. The smallest kidney function unit is the uriniferous cone, each containing a nephron and a collection tube. Approximately 1 and 1.3 million nephrons are present in each kidney. One nephron is later composed of part of the blood vessels (glomerulus), part of the fluid (Bowman's capsule), proximal tubule, Henley's loop, and distal tubule. Several nephrons are drawn by a single collection tube, which grows downstream and into the Bellini canal and spreads the papilla of the kidneys. Extensive kidney function removes toxic products of metabolism and other molecules smaller than 69000 Da (i.e., smaller than albumin) by filtering the blood flowing through glomerulus. They also regulate body composition and body fluids. Specifically, the reuptake of salts (Na +, K +, Cl-), glucose, creatine, protein and water occurs in chunks. As a result of this removal and conservation function, the kidneys also contribute to the regulation of blood pressure, hemodynamics, and acid-base balance. In addition, the kidneys have endocrine function: They produce the hormones rennin, erythropoietin, and prostaglandin and help convert Vitamin D into dihydroxycholecalciferol, a substance that regulates calcium transport.

Blood Constitution and Major Jobs

The average adult has a blood volume of about 5l, which is about 7% of total body weight. Blood is a diamond to pump black blood. The primary functions of the blood system include the transfer of nutrients from the gastrointestinal tract to all cells of the body and thereafter, delivering the waste of these cells to the digestive organs. Oxygen (O2) is taken from the lungs to all cells in the body by hemoglobin to erythrocytes, while carbon dioxide (CO2) is returned to the lungs to be removed by hemoglobin and plasma. In addition to nutrients, blood flow also carries many other metabolites, cellular products, and electrolytes. In addition, the blood has the function of regulating body temperature and maintaining acid-base and osmotic balance of body fluids. Plasma contains water (90%), protein (9%) and inorganic salts, ions, nitrogen, nutrients, and gases (1%). Many plasma proteins exist have different origins and functions (e.g., albumin (69000 Da), bglobulins (80000-1E-6 Da), g-globulins, clotting proteins, and related proteins plasma lipoproteins). In regions with high oxygen concentration, a portion of the hemoglobin releases CO2 while the iron binds to O2. Leukocytes use blood flow as a means of transport and only perform their function after diapedesis. Inside the bloodstream, leukocytes are round, while pleomorphic in connective tissue. Their main function is to protect the human body from external influences. They can be divided into two main groups: granulocytes (60-70% neutrophiles, 4% nosinophiles, and 1% basophiles) and agranulocytes (20-25% lymphocytes and 3-8% monocytes). Uremic Syndrome

Kidney dysfunction can be divided into three stages in which the kidneys lose their ability to cleanse the blood: acute (hours to days), subacute (months) and chronic kidney failure (years). In contrast to subacute and chronic form, severe kidney failure is often reversible. Uremic syndrome is the result of chemical retention, which is often performed by healthy kidneys, as well as disruption of hormonal and enzymatic homeostasis. As kidney failure progresses, the level of glomerular filtration and the number of active nephrons decreases. The main causes of renal disease are chronic diabetes and high blood pressure, and the most important symptoms are found in the cardiovascular, neurological, hematological, and immune systems.

Renal Replacement Therapies

Diagnosis of chronic kidney failure is based on indications of decreased kidney function or urinary incontinence. In daily practice, creatinine clearance (95720 ml / min for women and 120725 ml / min for men) is used as a measure of glomerular ultrafiltration and measures the remaining kidney function. Ureum removal, in contrast to creatinine clearance, is highly dependent on protein intake, in the patient’s advanced condition and level of urine flow. However, an increase in serum ureum is an additional useful symptom of chronic kidney failure. As with kidney replacement therapy, three methods are available: kidney transplant from cadaver or live donors, peritoneal dialysis, and hemodialysis. We'll look at the last 2 in this conversation.

Peritoneal Dialysis

With peritoneal dialysis, hypertonic glucose dialysis fluid is inserted into the peritoneal cavity using a permanent peritoneal tube. The peritoneal cavity is the inner space and abdomen surrounded by a serous membrane called the peritoneum (1-1.5 m2). The inevitable membrane that contains mesothelial cells in the interstitium contains tissues that connect capillaries and lymphatic vessels. Among mesothelial cells, intercellular spaces (range 5 nm) are responsible for the mass transfer of solute between dialysis fluid and blood to capillaries. Ultrafiltration is mainly achieved by the osmotic effect of glucose, poly-glucose, or other osmotic agents on dialysate, and occurs through very small pores (diameter 0.5 nm), also called aquaporin. Solute removal and ultrafiltration are mainly determined by the availability of peritoneal membranes. This feature is patient-dependent and can be calculated with a peritoneum equilibration test (PET). After a 2-14 hour stay, the peritoneal cavity is removed and filled with a cycling machine or by hand with the flow of gravity. To assist the patient with fluid exchange, various easy-to-use and automatic equipment is available along with devices to ensure proper, safe and sterile communication with bags or machine. Peritoneal dialysis can be performed continuously (CAPD-Continuous Ambulatory Peritoneal Dialysis, CCPD-Continuous Cyclic Peritoneal Dialysis) or periodically (DAPD-Daytime Ambulatory Peritoneal Dialysis, IPD-Intermittent Peritoneal Dialysis, NIPD - Nightly Intermittent Peritone

Hemodialysis is a medical procedure that involves the bleaching of a patient's blood through a transplant kidney, also called hemodialyzer. This procedure is performed in an external circuit where one or two needles (or catheters) can be used as access to the patient's arteries. Typical treatment for hemodialysis lasts about 9-15 hours a week, mostly spreading more than three times. It could be in the hospital, in the intensive care unit or at home. Two types of hemodialyzers are used: plate and fiber dialyzers. In a plate dialyzer, membrane sheets are collected together and blood and dialysate flow to the next layer. The priming capacity is almost 30% larger than an empty dialyzer. The latter contains thousands of small capillaries (inner diameter 200 mm and wall thickness 8-40 mm). Blood flows inside the capillaries while dialysate flows against them currently around them. The normal blood flow rate is in the range of 200 to 350 ml / min, and dialysate flow selects twice the blood flow. In addition to the benefits of small blood volume, these dialyzers experience problems such as closure and closure of capillaries. Dialysis Fluid Characteristics

Hemodialysis fluid should be considered a temporary extension of the fluid outside the patient's cells due to the process of transfusion on both sides where blood and dialysate flow through the dialyzer. It contains reverse osmosis, dextrose, and various electrolytes such as calcium chloride, magnesium chloride, potassium chloride and sodium-chloride and sodium acetate or sodium bicarbonate. The latter two fulfill the function of dialysate buffer, which is responsible for the correction of metabolic acidosis in uremic patients.
